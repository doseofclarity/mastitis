Mastitis

Acute inflammation and infection of the breast during lactation. Master etiology, risk factors, clinical signs, antibiotic therapy, breastfeeding management, and prevention.

Definition & Etiology

Mastitis is an inflammation of the breast tissue, often caused by infection, that commonly occurs during lactation. It typically affects one breast and peaks between 2–4 weeks postpartum, though it can occur at any time during breastfeeding.
  • Etiologic agent: Staphylococcus aureus (most common), also Streptococcus, E. coli.
  • Entry portal: Cracked or fissured nipples → bacteria from infant's nose/pharynx or mother's skin enters breast tissue.
  • Predisposing factor: Milk stasis (incomplete emptying, missed feedings, poor latch) → allows bacterial proliferation.

Risk Factors & Pathophysiology

👩 Risk Factors
• Cracked or sore nipples
• Poor latch or positioning
• Missed feedings or abrupt weaning
• Overly tight bra or clothing
• Maternal stress, fatigue
• Previous mastitis history
• Use of nipple creams without proper hygiene
🔬 Pathophysiology
Milk stasis → bacterial overgrowth → inflammatory response → edema, pain, erythema. If untreated, may progress to abscess formation. Inflammation can lead to systemic symptoms (fever, chills, malaise).

Clinical Manifestations & Diagnosis

  • Local signs: Breast pain, tenderness, erythema (wedge‑shaped area), warmth, swelling.
  • Systemic signs: Fever (>38.5°C), chills, malaise, myalgia, flu‑like symptoms.
  • Timing: Usually unilateral (rarely bilateral).
  • Diagnosis: Clinical – based on history and physical exam. If recurrent or severe, milk culture and antibiotic sensitivity testing. Ultrasound to rule out abscess.
  • Differential diagnosis: Breast engorgement (bilateral, no fever/infection), plugged duct (localized tender lump, afebrile), inflammatory breast cancer (rare).
⚠️ Key distinction: Engorgement: bilateral, full breasts, afebrile. Mastitis: unilateral, erythema, fever, systemic symptoms.

Treatment & Management

  • Antibiotics: Dicloxacillin or cephalexin (first‑line) – cover S. aureus. For penicillin‑allergic: clindamycin or erythromycin. Duration: 10–14 days; symptoms improve in 24–48 hours but complete course essential.
  • Breastfeeding / Milk expression: Continue breastfeeding or pumping on the affected side – emptying the breast is crucial. Start feeding on the unaffected side to let down reflex, then switch to affected side. If too painful, pump.
  • Supportive measures: Analgesics (acetaminophen or ibuprofen), adequate hydration, rest, supportive bra. Warm compresses before feeding to promote milk flow; cold compresses after feeding for comfort.
  • Abscess management: If abscess forms (fluctuant mass), needle aspiration or surgical incision and drainage. Continue antibiotics. Temporary pumping from affected breast may be advised.
✅ Key teaching: Breastfeeding is safe for the infant – bacteria are not harmful; antibiotics are safe for breastfeeding.

Nursing Role & Patient Education

  • Prevention: Teach proper breastfeeding technique (good latch, positioning), avoid skipped feedings, treat cracked nipples early, hand‑express to soften breast before feeding.
  • Early recognition: Educate on signs (pain, redness, fever) – prompt treatment prevents abscess.
  • Medication teaching: Take antibiotics as prescribed, complete full course, do not discontinue early. Use analgesics as needed.
  • Breast care: Apply warm compresses before feeding, cold packs after. Use supportive bra, avoid tight clothing.
  • Rest and hydration: Encourage rest, increase fluid intake.
  • Follow‑up: If no improvement in 48–72 hours, notify provider (possible abscess or resistant organism).
